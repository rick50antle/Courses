# Standard for Teaching Nonprofit Accounting

**Conceptual Foundations, Terminology, and Explanatory Conventions**

Rick Antle · Yale School of Management · Draft — February 2026

---

> This standard governs how nonprofit accounting concepts are explained in all teaching artifacts — interactive tutorials, presentations, case studies, and exercises. It ensures conceptual precision, consistent terminology, and explanatory sequences that build genuine understanding rather than surface familiarity.

---

## Part I: Foundational Principles

### 1. Accounting as a Human-Created Information System

Every teaching artifact must establish that accounting is a human-created information system, not a mirror of economic reality. Accounting records show recorded amounts based on rules humans have chosen. Many economically valuable things — brand reputation, skilled staff, community relationships, donor networks — are not included in the recorded amounts.

This principle should be surfaced at least once in every artifact, and should be reinforced whenever a concept risks being mistaken for a description of economic reality. The gap between recorded amounts and economic value is not a flaw to be apologized for; it is a design feature that reflects deliberate choices about what can be reliably measured.

> **Key Framing:** Accounting representations ≠ economic reality. The Statement of Financial Position shows recorded assets, which necessarily exclude valuable things that cannot be reliably estimated — and the reasons for that exclusion (verifiability, comparability, conservatism, cost-benefit) should be taught explicitly.

### 2. The Accounting Identity as the Entry Point

Nonprofit accounting concepts are introduced through the accounting identity, not through standalone definitions. The identity is:

**Assets − Liabilities = ?**

The **?** becomes whatever we *name* the difference. This name depends on the type of entity. The right-hand side of the identity is a **DEFINITIONAL** residual — it is not an independent thing with its own economic meaning. It is the name we give to the mathematical difference between recorded assets and recorded liabilities.

### 3. For-Profit as the Bridge

New nonprofit concepts are always introduced through explicit comparison to their for-profit counterpart. The audience is assumed to know for-profit accounting; nonprofit concepts are framed as departures from that known foundation.

| AVOID | PREFERRED |
|---|---|
| "Nonprofits use a Statement of Financial Position, which shows assets, liabilities, and net assets." | "Like a for-profit balance sheet, the Statement of Financial Position shows assets and liabilities. But the difference between them is called Net Assets rather than Equities, because there are no owners — accountability runs to mission and donors." |

### 4. Explain the "Why" Behind the Rules

Every accounting rule or standard cited in a teaching artifact must come with a reason a practitioner, board member, or student would care about it. Rules are never stated as arbitrary requirements. The human motivations — verifiability, comparability, conservatism, cost-benefit, accountability — should be made explicit.

## Part II: How to Explain Net Assets

### 5. The Required Explanatory Sequence

Net Assets must be introduced in the following sequence. Skipping steps or reordering them leads to misunderstanding.

1. **Identity.** Start from Assets − Liabilities = ?. Establish the mathematical relationship first.
2. **Name.** The ? is whatever we name the difference. For for-profits: Equities (plural — a DEFINITIONAL term). For nonprofits: Net Assets.
3. **Why the name differs.** Nonprofits have no owners. Net Assets shows the sources of funding and the status of donor restrictions, not ownership claims.
4. **Internal structure.** Net Assets are broken into two categories: With Donor Restrictions and Without Donor Restrictions.
5. **The critical subtlety.** Board designations remain in Without Donor Restrictions because only donors can create binding restrictions. The board can change its mind.
6. **Practical consequence.** You cannot assess financial flexibility from the total alone. The composition of net assets — and what the underlying assets actually are — is what matters.

### 6. What Net Asset Categories Actually Track

The two net asset categories track the status of donor restrictions on contributions. They are an information system about whether donor-imposed restrictions existed when contributions were recorded, and whether those restrictions have been satisfied.

> **Critical Distinction**
>
> **Net Assets With Donor Restrictions:** Contributions came in with donor-imposed restrictions that have NOT yet been met.
>
> **Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions:** Either the contributions never had donor restrictions, OR they had restrictions that HAVE been satisfied and were reclassified (released).

The "released from restrictions" line on the Statement of Activities shows donor-restricted funds where the restriction was met during the period. The amount moves from With Donor Restrictions to Without Donor Restrictions. Total net assets do not change — it is a reclassification.

### 7. What Net Asset Categories Do NOT Tell You

The net asset categories do not describe spending capacity. They do not tell you how much money is "available for general use." Net Assets is the definitional residual of the accounting identity, not a pool of cash. An organization could have large Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions tied up entirely in buildings, equipment, or other illiquid assets with almost no cash available.

| AVOID | PREFERRED |
|---|---|
| "Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions are available for general use." | "Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions indicates that the accumulated contributions in this category either had no donor restrictions or had restrictions that have been satisfied. It does not tell you what resources are liquid or available to spend." |

## Part III: Terminology Standards

### 8. "Reserves"

The word "reserves" is not an accounting term under nonprofit standards and should be **avoided** in teaching artifacts. When it appears in practice, it conflates the accounting classification (which tracks restriction status) with actual liquidity or spending capacity.

If "reserves" is used in reference to board-designated net assets, the standard requires clarification that a board designation is a statement of intent, not a segregation of resources. The board has expressed an intention for how it would like to use some portion of the organization's resources. The board can change that intention at any meeting. No money has been moved or locked away.

> **Terminology Hierarchy**
>
> **Donor restrictions:** Binding external constraints. Track whether the organization has met obligations imposed by donors.
>
> **Board designations:** Internal statements of intent. Remain classified within Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions. The board can change its mind.
>
> **"Reserves":** Misleading. Implies a pool of segregated, spendable money, which misrepresents both what board designations are and what net asset classifications do.

### 9. Precise Definitions

Definitions in teaching artifacts use conceptual framing, not dictionary entries. Each definition includes emphasis cues (capitalization for key conceptual words) and parenthetical clarifications to preempt common misconceptions.

| Term | Required Framing |
|---|---|
| **Asset** | The present RIGHT to an economic benefit (not a physical thing, but a right to receive future benefits) |
| **Liability** | A present OBLIGATION to transfer an economic benefit to another party |
| **Equities** | For for-profit entities: the DEFINITIONAL difference between recorded assets and liabilities. Note: plural form is deliberate. |
| **Net Assets** | For nonprofit entities: the DEFINITIONAL difference between recorded assets and liabilities, showing the status of donor restrictions on accumulated contributions. |
| **Contribution** | An unconditional transfer of cash or other assets in a voluntary nonreciprocal transfer (donor receives no direct benefit in return). |
| **Board Designation** | A statement of intent by the board for specific uses of unrestricted resources (NOT a binding restriction — the board can change its mind). |

## Part IV: Financial Statement Explanations

### 10. The Three Statements as a Connected System

The three required nonprofit financial statements must always be taught as a connected system, never as independent reports. Good analysis connects all three.

- **Statement of Financial Position:** Position at a point in time. Shows recorded assets, liabilities, and the composition of net assets.
- **Statement of Activities:** Changes in net assets during a period. Explains what caused net assets to change and tracks the movement between restricted and unrestricted categories.
- **Statement of Cash Flows:** A reconciliation (under the indirect method) between the change in net assets from the Statement of Activities and the change in cash on the Statement of Financial Position.

Every artifact that covers more than one statement should demonstrate cross-statement articulation — showing how a number on one statement connects to or verifies a number on another.

### 11. The Cash Flow Statement as a Reconciliation

When the indirect method is used, the cash flow statement must be framed as a reconciliation, not a freestanding report. It answers the question: "The Statement of Activities says net assets changed by $X, but cash only changed by $Y — what accounts for the difference?"

The starting point is the change in total net assets (from the Statement of Activities). The ending point is the change in cash (verifiable against the Statement of Financial Position). Everything in between explains why those two numbers differ: non-cash items like depreciation, timing differences in payables and receivables, and investing and financing activities.

> **Teaching Note:** The indirect method cash flow statement operates at the total net assets level. It is indifferent to the composition of net assets between restricted and unrestricted categories. Organizations with very different donor restriction profiles but identical total changes will produce identical cash flow statements. (The Uncle Pennybags exercise demonstrates this directly.)

### 12. The Statement of Functional Expenses

The Statement of Functional Expenses is unique to nonprofits. It shows how expenses are allocated among program services, management and general, and fundraising. Teaching artifacts should address the judgment involved in allocation (especially shared costs), the importance of consistency over time, and why donors and watchdog organizations pay attention to the ratio of program spending to total spending.

## Part V: Liquidity and Financial Flexibility

### 13. Liquidity Is an Asset-Side Question

When someone asks "Do we have enough to keep operating?" the answer lives in liquidity analysis, not in the net asset categories. Liquidity is about which assets can be converted to cash to meet near-term obligations. It is an asset-side question.

The net asset categories tell you about the status of donor restrictions. They do not tell you whether the organization can pay its bills. An organization could have $100 million in Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions and still face a liquidity crisis if those net assets are embodied in a building, a collection, and long-term receivables.

| AVOID | PREFERRED |
|---|---|
| "The organization has strong reserves — Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions are $20 million." | "Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions are $20 million, but the liquidity footnote shows only $3.2 million in financial assets available within one year to meet general expenditures." |

### 14. The Required Liquidity Footnote

ASC 958-210 requires nonprofits to disclose qualitative and quantitative information about how they manage liquidity and the availability of financial assets to meet cash needs for general expenditures within one year. Teaching artifacts should direct attention to this footnote as the place that answers the question people think "reserves" answers.

The footnote tells you which financial assets are available within one year and what constraints limit their availability — donor restrictions, board designations, the illiquid nature of certain assets. It is the proper tool for assessing whether the organization can sustain operations, not the net asset classification on the face of the statements.

## Part VI: Pedagogical Standards

### 15. Progressive Revelation

Teaching artifacts should build from simple to complex, with each step building on the previous one. Start with concrete calculations before introducing conceptual analysis. The pattern: calculate first, name second, interpret third, apply fourth.

Where possible, use progressive revelation to create insight — showing identical surfaces that conceal important differences (as in the Uncle Pennybags exercise, where five organizations with identical balance sheets have very different net asset compositions and therefore very different financial stories).

### 16. Calculation Paired with Interpretation

Every quantitative exercise must include an interpretation step. Students should never perform a calculation without being asked what it means. "Calculate the percentage of restricted net assets" is incomplete; "Calculate the percentage and explain what it means for the organization's financial flexibility" is complete.

### 17. Real Organizations, Real Numbers

Teaching artifacts should use actual audited financial statements from real nonprofit organizations, or realistic constructed data with plausible financial structures. Avoid toy examples with round numbers that don't feel like real financial statements. When real organizations are used, provide context about their mission and operations so students understand what the numbers represent.

### 18. Three-Level Hint Scaffolding

Interactive artifacts use a consistent three-level hint structure:

1. **Nudge:** A gentle push toward the right approach without giving the answer.
2. **Guidance:** More specific direction, possibly identifying the relevant calculation or concept.
3. **Full answer:** The complete answer with explanation, shown after two unsuccessful attempts or on request.

### 19. AI Transparency

Any artifact that uses AI (for evaluation, tutoring, or content generation) must include a clear disclaimer acknowledging: that it is AI-powered, that AI systems can make mistakes, that it is a learning aid and not a replacement for classroom instruction, and that students should verify important concepts with their instructor or course materials.

## Part VII: Visual and Formatting Conventions

### 20. Color Coding

Teaching artifacts use consistent color coding for callout boxes and highlighted information:

- **Blue:** Key information, data presentations, and explanatory context.
- **Yellow:** Important warnings, critical distinctions, and common misconceptions to avoid.
- **Green:** Correct answers, verifications, and teaching notes.
- **Purple:** Summaries and synthesis of multiple concepts.
- **Red:** Critical rules and the most important distinctions (e.g., "Only DONORS can create binding restrictions").

### 21. Side-by-Side Comparisons

When contrasting for-profit and nonprofit treatments, or when showing correct vs. incorrect framings, use side-by-side visual presentations. Red highlighting or boxing should draw attention to where the key differences lie.

### 22. Emphasis Conventions

Use ALL CAPS sparingly and only for key conceptual words that carry definitional weight: DEFINITIONAL, RIGHT, OBLIGATION. Use bold for terms being defined or for critical rules. Use italics for terms being contrasted with their for-profit counterparts.

## Appendix: Quick Reference — Common Errors to Avoid

| Common Error | Correct Framing |
|---|---|
| "Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions are available for general use." | Net Asset categories track the status of donor restrictions, not spending capacity. Resources may be illiquid. |
| "The organization has $X in reserves." | "Reserves" is not an accounting term. Specify what is meant: board-designated net assets (a statement of intent), liquid financial assets, or something else. |
| Board designations are a type of restriction. | Only donors can create binding restrictions. Board designations are statements of intent that remain in Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions. |
| Net Assets is like equity — it represents ownership. | Net Assets is the DEFINITIONAL difference between recorded assets and liabilities. Nonprofits have no owners. Net Assets tracks the status of donor restrictions. |
| The cash flow statement shows where the money came from and went. | Under the indirect method, the cash flow statement is a reconciliation between the change in net assets and the change in cash. |
| Large Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions means the organization is financially healthy. | Financial health requires examining liquidity (the asset side and the required footnote), not just net asset categories. The organization may have large unrestricted net assets locked in illiquid form. |
