# Core Pedagogical Standards

**Version:** 1.2
**Date:** February 8, 2026
**Purpose:** Defines terminology, approach, and pedagogical requirements that apply to all accounting tutorial projects

## Terminology Requirements

### Measurement

The word "measurement" (and derivatives like "measuring," "measured") is never used when discussing accounting processes. "Measurement" implies objective, single-dimensional capture — like measuring height. Accounting involves multi-faceted economic phenomena reduced to monetary representations through conventions, judgments, and estimates.

| Never Use | Use Instead | When |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| measurement | **estimation** | Emphasizing judgment and uncertainty |
| measure | **quantification** | Emphasizing the process of assigning numbers |
| measured at | **accounting valuation** | Discussing asset or liability amounts |
| measuring | **assessment** | Discussing evaluation processes |
| measured by | **allocation** | Discussing depreciation, amortization, and similar topics |

When a student uses "measurement," gently reframe without being pedantic. Corrections should educate about the multi-dimensional nature of economic reality being reduced to numbers, not just enforce vocabulary.

The word "measurement" is widely used in practice. Acknowledge this when relevant, then explain why this project avoids it.

### Accounting Identity

Always say **accounting identity**, never "accounting equation." The identity is a framework for recording, organizing, and reporting financial information — not a formula to memorize.

The general form starts with:

**Assets − Liabilities = ?**

The name given to the difference depends on the type of entity:

- For-profit: Assets − Liabilities = **Equities** (plural form is deliberate — it is a DEFINITIONAL residual, not a single coherent "thing")
- Not-for-profit: Assets − Liabilities = **Net Assets**

Whatever name is given to this difference, its total amount is mathematically and completely determined by the specification of the total amounts of Assets and Liabilities. It is a residual.

**Critical constraint on Equities:** When used in an accounting identity, the meaning of Equities is necessarily limited by the accounting rules and conventions that limit the recording of Assets and Liabilities. Because not all assets and liabilities are recorded by accountants, and because their book values may not equal cash realizable or settlement amounts, Equities is almost never what is left for the owners — except in the special case where all assets have been converted to cash and all nonowner claims are settleable for a known amount of cash and are recorded.

### Equities Terminology Rules

When discussing equities in any teaching artifact:

1. **Always use "Equities" (plural)**, not "Equity" (singular). The plural form reinforces that this is a definitional residual — a category label — not a single coherent economic thing.
2. **Always emphasize it is a DEFINITIONAL difference.** Equities = Assets − Liabilities, by definition.
3. **Never frame equities as "what's left for owners."** That framing is only accurate in the special liquidation case described above. In normal operations, book equities reflect recorded amounts under accounting conventions — not the owners' economic wealth.
4. **Clarify that negative book equities does not mean a worthless company is not necessarily the case.** Unrecognized economic assets (brand value, customer relationships, human capital) can make an entity valuable even when the accounting records show negative equities.

### Measure (in the sense of standard/comparison)

Distinct from "measurement" above, the word "measure" also creates problems when used to describe a common standard for comparison (e.g., "barley measures the value of wool"). Three specific issues:

1. **It implies precision and objectivity** — A "measure" suggests something fixed and reliable, like a meter stick. But a common standard (such as barley-as-standard) is neither fixed nor objective; its supply fluctuates.

2. **It obscures the relational nature of exchange** — When someone says "barley measures the value of wool," students may think the standard *determines* or *creates* value. In reality, exchange relationships exist independently; the standard just makes them *visible*.

3. **It conflates the lens with what's being seen** — When the standard's supply shifts, all "prices" move even though real exchange relationships have not changed. If students think of the standard as a "measuring stick," they may conclude the stick is broken rather than understanding that the lens distorted.

| Never Use | Use Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| common measure | **common standard** / **standard for comparison** |
| measuring stick | **standard** / **lens** |
| measured in X | **expressed in X** / **in X terms** |
| to measure value | **to compare** / **to see relationships** |

The word "value" (as a noun) and "worth" also require care. Prefer language that emphasizes what something can *command* in exchange rather than implying an intrinsic property:

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| the value of X | **what X can command** / **what you can get for X** |
| X is worth 30 barley | **X commands 30 barley** / **X trades for 30 barley** |
| barley was worth less | **barley could get you less** / **each unit of barley commanded less** |

**Key phrases to use:**
- "The **common standard** makes comparison possible"
- "Expressing everything in a common standard lets you **see** relationships that were hidden"
- "The standard is a **lens** for viewing exchange relationships — not the source of them"
- "What can X **command** from others?"

**Pedagogical rationale:** The goal is to help learners see that the common standard is a **tool for seeing** exchange relationships, not the **source** of those relationships. When the standard itself shifts, the lens distorts — but the underlying exchange relationships have not changed. The numbers moved, but nothing real did.

### General Language

- Never use the royal **"we"** in a response.
- Prefer plain language over academic jargon.
- Use active voice to show human agency: "Accountants choose..." not "It is determined..."
- Always qualify institutional statements: "GAAP requires..." not "We must..."

| Academic/Technical | Plain Language |
|-------------------|----------------|
| cohere / coherent | line up / consistent |
| numeraire | common standard |
| commensurable | able to compare / in the same terms |
| unit of account | common standard |

## The Fundamental Distinction Principle

Every tutorial must consistently distinguish between:

1. **Accounting representations** — What appears in the books, statements, and records
2. **Economic reality** — The actual resources, obligations, and activities of the entity

### Required Language Precision

| Never Say | Say Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| The company's assets are... | The balance sheet reports assets of... |
| The company has $X in equity | The accounting records show equities of $X |
| Assets equal... | Recorded assets total... |
| Recognized in the accounts | Recorded in the accounts (then note what's missing) |

### Required Behaviors

1. **Flag gaps explicitly.** When introducing any financial statement item, note what it captures AND what it misses. Example: "The balance sheet shows $2M in recorded assets, but this excludes the company's brand reputation and skilled workforce."

2. **Use precise language.** The records *show* things; the entity *has* things. These are not the same.

3. **Provide reality checks.** Regularly remind learners that accounting is an information gathering, processing, and reporting system with rules and limitations, not a perfect mirror of economic reality.

4. **Highlight common exclusions.** Frequently mention examples:
   - Internally generated intangibles (brands, customer relationships)
   - Human capital
   - Market position advantages
   - Contingent assets not yet probable

5. **Explain the real dynamics.** Provide honest context about why certain economic assets are not recorded:
   - **Human motives** — Accounting rules are created by people with interests. Companies may prefer not to record certain obligations or may benefit from keeping valuable assets off the books.
   - **Power and politics** — Standard-setters face lobbying pressure. Rules often reflect compromises between competing interests, not pure logic.
   - **Conflict avoidance** — Some conventions exist to prevent disputes. Historical cost persists partly because it avoids constant revaluation arguments.
   - **Cost-benefit calculations** — Recording and auditing information costs money. Some items stay off the books because the cost of inclusion exceeds perceived benefits to those making the rules.
   - **Strategic concealment** — Managers may prefer opacity around certain resources or obligations for competitive or self-interested reasons.

## Teaching Approach

### Experience First, Name Later

Learners must experience a concept before learning its technical name. They should feel the problem, work through the implications, and discover why a solution is needed — then learn what the formal system calls it.

**Pattern:**
1. Learner encounters a problem or phenomenon
2. Learner works through the implications
3. Learner discovers why a systematic approach is needed
4. THEN introduce the formal term: "This process has a name: **[term]**."

Never introduce technical terms in preview boxes or "What You'll Discover" sections before learners have experienced the concept.

### Critical Perspective

Present accounting as a human-designed system with embedded interests, not as natural law. Never present accounting rules as objective truth. Never imply current standards are optimal or inevitable.

When explaining any rule or standard:
1. Identify who made the rule and when
2. Explain what interests were at play
3. Discuss what alternatives were rejected and why
4. Note who benefits and who bears costs from this choice

### Structured Concept Delivery

For each major topic, follow this pattern:
1. **Economic phenomenon** — What actually happens
2. **Information challenge** — Why capturing this in numbers is difficult
3. **Accounting response** — The conventional solution chosen
4. **Hidden interests** — Who benefits from this particular solution
5. **Alternatives** — Other ways this could be handled

### Double-Entry Emphasis

Wherever possible, emphasize the importance of double-entry accounting, its role in the accounting process, and that all numbers reported on financial statements get there only by double-entry. The genius of double-entry recordkeeping lies in how it can be used in conjunction with an identity to convey **information** about recorded Assets and Liabilities — typically information related to the transaction, event, or circumstance that gave rise to the change.

### Definitions

An **asset** is "the present right to an economic benefit." An economic **benefit** is anything useful to the entity in accomplishing its mission, whether that mission is to earn a profit, feed the hungry, or maintain city roads.

A **liability** is "a present obligation of an entity to transfer an economic benefit." An **obligation** is not optional — it must be fulfilled. An **obligation** is "any condition that binds an entity to some performance or action."

### Question and Explanation Design Checklist

Every question in a teaching artifact must:

- Distinguish "shows/records" from "actually has" — never conflate accounting representations with economic reality
- Use "equities" (plural) when referring to the for-profit residual, not "equity"
- Avoid "measurement" and all derivatives — use the approved alternatives
- Include reality checks about what the accounting records exclude and why
- Frame accounting rules as human choices with identifiable motives

Every explanation must:

- Separate book values from economic values
- Note what is excluded from the accounting records and why
- Use precise accounting language (records *show*; entities *have*)
- Include the human motivations behind the rules being discussed

### Prohibited Behaviors

- Never present accounting rules as objective truth
- Never use textbook cliches like "true and fair view" without critique
- Never suggest accounting is primarily about "helping stakeholders"
- Never imply current standards are optimal or inevitable
- Never use the royal "we"
