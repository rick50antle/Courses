# Terminology and Language Conventions

## CMU METALS Prework Redesign

*Last updated: March 18, 2026*

This document collects language decisions — words and phrases to use, and words and phrases to avoid — so that all drafting is consistent. These are not style preferences. Each one reflects a substantive intellectual commitment about what accounting is and how we talk about it.

---

## Active Constraints

### Never use "measurement" or "measure"

Accounting does not measure. A scale measures weight — a single, well-defined physical property that exists independent of the instrument. Accounting quantifies, estimates, expresses, assesses, allocates. Each of these words carries an honest acknowledgment that judgment and convention are involved.

**Use instead:** express, quantify, estimate, assess, allocate

---

### Never use "need" when describing the user's relationship to understanding

"Need" implies compulsion — it takes away the element of choice. People choose to understand complex systems, and they make better choices when they do. The prework should respect the student's agency, not conscript them.

**Use instead:** "want to understand," "choose to understand," or restructure the sentence to remove the implication of requirement entirely.

**Example:**
- Wrong: "Many of the things we need to understand aren't well-defined properties."
- Right: "Many of the things we want to understand aren't well-defined properties."

---

### Never use "the answer" or imply that the system produces answers

An accounting system produces structured representations — reports, statements, numbers. These are outputs, not answers. Calling them answers implies that the system resolves questions on its own. It does not. The outputs require interpretation by a person who understands what the system did and what it left out. The system informs judgment; it does not replace it.

**Use instead:** "outputs," "reports," "representations," "information." When referring to what users do with outputs: "consult," "interpret," "work with," "draw on."

**Example:**
- Wrong: "The system provides answers about the financial health of the organization."
- Right: "The system produces reports that inform judgments about the financial health of the organization."
- Wrong: "Different users need different answers from the system."
- Right: "Different users come to the same system with different questions."

---

### Use "worth" in preference to "value"

"Value" sounds precise and definitive. "Worth" is softer, more honest — it carries the implication that reasonable people might disagree, that markets are incomplete, that the number is a convention, not a fact. This is exactly right for what accounting does.

**Use instead:** "worth" in most contexts where "value" would appear.

**Exception:** "Fair value" is a technical term in accounting standards (ASC 820, IFRS 13) and should be used when referring to that specific concept. Similarly, "book value" and "net realizable value" are standard terms.

---

### Money "expresses" worth — it does not "measure" it

This is a specific application of the no-measurement rule, but important enough to state separately. A ruler measures length — the length exists independent of the ruler. Money does not work that way. Money is a shared convention, a common language for comparing things that trade in markets. It is a reference point, not a natural law.

---

### The four financial statements are "four windows onto one structure"

Never describe the financial statements as four independent or separate documents. They are four views of the same underlying data, each designed to show what the others cannot show directly. A sophisticated reader uses them together.

---

### Historical cost is the "strongest epistemic anchor for worth"

An arm's-length transaction is direct market evidence — two independent parties looked at the same thing and agreed on a price. That agreement is a market. That price is worth expressed. Do not describe historical cost as merely "verifiable but potentially stale." Describe what it is: the most direct evidence of market worth available at the moment of transaction.

---

### Say "accounting systems," not "accounting"

"Accounting" is abstract — it sounds like a subject, a discipline, a body of rules. "Accounting systems" is concrete — it points to designed artifacts that exist in the world, that take inputs and produce outputs, that were built by people to serve purposes. It also prepares the ground for the architecture/instance distinction: there are many accounting systems (instances), and they share a common architecture. Use "accounting systems" when referring to the general family. Use a specific name (US GAAP, IFRS, etc.) when referring to a particular instance.

**Example:**
- Too vague: "Accounting belongs to this family."
- Better: "Accounting systems belong to this family."

---

### The accounting identity is "imposed," not "derived" or "discovered"

The identity is a design choice — a deliberate structural constraint that makes the information system internally consistent, auditable, and useful. It was not derived from economic theory. It was not found in nature. It was chosen because of what imposing it makes possible.

---

### We are "helping them learn," not "teaching them"

The prework does not deliver a complete package for the student to receive. It starts them on a journey. The student is doing the work; we are providing structure and a path. "Teach" puts us in the active role and the student in the passive one. "Help them learn" keeps the student as the agent.

**Use instead:** "help you learn," "start you on," "introduce," "begin." Avoid "teach you," "show you how," or any phrasing that implies the prework delivers mastery.

**Example:**
- Wrong: "This prework teaches you how the system works."
- Right: "That is what this prework is about." Or: "This prework is where that journey begins."

## Tone and Register

### Write as if explaining in conversation

The target register is how you would explain something to a smart person in your office — warm, intellectually serious, no jargon without purpose, no abstraction without a concrete example nearby.

### Never present a rule without its tradeoff

Every design choice in the system gains something and loses something. The student should always see both sides.

### Never present a number without asking what it captures and what it omits

This is the habit the prework is helping students to build. Model it consistently.
