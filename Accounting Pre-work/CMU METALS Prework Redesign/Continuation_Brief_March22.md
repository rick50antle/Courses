## What Was Done This Session

### 1. Corrected Context-Compaction Error

The previous session's continuation brief contained a fabricated objective title — "Distinguish stocks from flows and locate each in the identity" — for what it called Objective 11. This title does not appear in Learning_Objectives.md and was generated during context compaction. The correct mapping was restored by going back to the source document.

### 2. Completed the One-by-One Title Interview

Continued the interview from where Session 3 left off (after Objective 10). The corrected mapping showed the remaining objectives were old 13–19, not old 12–18 as the compacted brief claimed.

**Four objectives were dropped** as too ambitious for prework:

- Old 15 / "Why cash gets its own statement" — subsumed by the new Objective 12 (structure of each decomposition) on the structural side, and by the NI-vs-CFO objectives on the interpretive side.
- Old 16 / "NI vs. CFO gap: trace it" — too much for prework; tracing specific sources of the gap is a course-level skill.
- Old 17 / "NI vs. CFO gap: interpret it" — too much for prework; interpreting persistent multi-period patterns is course material.
- Old 18 / "Convention spectrum (lightened)" — a bridge too far; ranking items by convention-dependence is course material.

### 3. Established "Flow" vs. "Decomposition" Terminology Convention

Added to Terminology_and_Language_Conventions.md. Key points:

- "Cash flow statement" is accurate — cash actually moves.
- Don't generalize "flow" to the other statements. A fair value write-up runs a gain through the income statement, but nothing flowed. Depreciation puts expense on the income statement, but nothing moved.
- When referring to the statements as a group: "the decompositions" or "the decomposition statements," not "the flow statements."
- Use "changes," "increases," and "decreases" — not "flows."

### 4. Combined Old Objectives 8 and 11

Old Objective 8 ("Appreciate why decomposing a total that is already determined by subtraction can still add information") and old Objective 11 ("Understand that decomposition can add information") were effectively the same idea at the title level. The equities-decomposition point (old 8) is a specific instance of the general principle (old 11). They were combined into a single objective with the title changed from "that" to "how":

**New Objective 11:** "Understand how decomposition can be used to add information."

The restated line carries both the equities-specific point and the balance-sheet-change point.

### 5. Revised Objective 9 (formerly 10)

Old title: "Trace a transaction through the identity and verify balance."

New title: **"Start to experience how the identity is helpful in understanding transactions and events."**

Key decision: "understanding" rather than "analyzing" — "analyzing" invites the accountant perspective, which is not what the prework is building. The student is using the identity to make sense of what happened, not to apply a technique.

### 6. Added New Objective 10 — Entry-Level Clarity vs. Statement-Level Complexity

**"Recognize that each entry captures what happened and why, but that understanding what the financial statements reveal requires knowing how they were put together."**

This objective names the gap between the clear cause-and-effect of individual entries and the complexity of aggregated financial statements. Aggregating cash stays concrete; aggregating into retained earnings absorbs convention-driven adjustments. This motivates why decomposition matters and why understanding conventions is essential for reading statements.

### 8. Prework Opening Idea

Rick proposed opening the prework with a concrete question: "An organization spent $1,000 in cash. Why?" The identity lets you capture both the cash decrease and the reason in one entry — either it got another asset, paid a liability, or bought something that was used up. This also frames the identity as a first step in dealing with complexity: understanding past actions and events. The student encounters the classification problem (asset or expense?) before knowing that's what it's called — experience first, name later.
