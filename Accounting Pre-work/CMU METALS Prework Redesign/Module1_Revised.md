# Module 1: Welcome and Orientation

*For: CMU METALS Capstone Team | Date: March 18, 2026 | Author: Rick Antle*

## Prose Narrative

### Framing for the METALS Team

Module 1 is the student's first impression of the entire prework. It must do several things simultaneously: welcome a diverse audience, establish what accounting is and why it exists, orient the student to the structure of the prework, and give them a roadmap so they know what to skip and what not to skip.

This module should feel like walking into a well-designed building — you immediately understand the layout, you know where you're going, and you're curious to explore.

The revised framing makes three intellectual moves in the opening minutes, before any mention of balance sheets or debits and credits.

### Move 1: Complexity and the Need for a System

The opening should not begin with what accounting *is*. It should begin with *why it exists*. The answer: complexity.

A sole proprietor running a lemonade stand does not need an accounting system. She can see her inventory. She knows what she paid for the lemons. She remembers who owes her money. Economic reality is small enough to hold in one person's head.

Now consider CVS Health — thousands of stores, millions of transactions per day, an insurance subsidiary, billions in debt, contractual covenants that trigger legal consequences if accounting numbers cross certain thresholds, regulators requiring specific disclosures on specific schedules, investors in dozens of countries making decisions based on quarterly reports. No human mind can hold all of that. No group of human minds can hold it without some shared system for recording, organizing, and communicating what happened.

That is why accounting exists. Not because someone thought it would be useful to keep records. Because economic reality, past a certain threshold of complexity, exceeds the cognitive capacity of the people who need to understand it. An information system becomes necessary — not optional, not merely helpful, but necessary — when the complexity of what you need to track outstrips what you can track unaided.

This is the foundational point. Every design choice in accounting — what to record, how to quantify it, when to recognize it, how to present it — is a response to this problem: how do you compress complex economic reality into a form that human minds can work with?

### Move 2: Accounting as an Information System

Once the complexity point is planted, the thesis: accounting is an information system. Like any information system, it has a definite structure:

- **Inputs**: Economic events — transactions, commitments, changes in conditions — enter the system. Not every economic event qualifies; the system has an input boundary, and understanding that boundary is one of the most important things a sophisticated user learns.

- **Processing**: The system records and organizes those inputs according to a disciplined architecture. At the center of that architecture is a structural constraint — the accounting identity — that enforces internal consistency. Every input must be processed in a way that maintains the constraint. This is not a burden; it is the source of the system's power.

- **Outputs**: The system produces structured reports — financial statements — that present different views of the same underlying data. These are not four independent documents. They are four windows onto one structure, each designed to answer different questions.

- **Users**: Specific people rely on those outputs for specific purposes. Investors deciding whether to buy or sell. Lenders deciding whether to extend credit and writing covenants that use accounting numbers as enforceable triggers. Managers evaluating performance and allocating resources. Regulators assessing compliance. Courts determining damages. The system was designed to serve these users — and the design choices reflect their competing demands.

The key pedagogical move: the student should understand from the first minutes that this is a *system* — designed by humans, for humans, to manage a specific problem (complexity). It is not a set of arbitrary rules. It is not a clerical skill. It is one of the most consequential intellectual technologies ever developed.

### Move 3: Architecture and Instance

The third move is the distinction that will organize the entire prework: the difference between the general architecture and a specific instance.

The general architecture is the deep structure shared by every accounting system on earth. The accounting identity. Double-entry recording. The principle that changes must be explained. The four output channels. The input boundary defined by what transacts in markets. These are architectural — they do not vary across systems.

An instance is what you get when a specific rule-making body makes specific choices for a specific class of entities. The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) makes choices for US for-profit entities — that instance is called US GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles). The International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) makes choices for entities in most of the rest of the world — that instance is called IFRS. The Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB) makes choices for US state and local governments. Each instance makes different decisions about recognition thresholds, quantification conventions, disclosure requirements, and presentation formats. But no instance can violate the architecture. The identity holds. Double-entry holds. The four windows remain.

The visual from the original draft still works here, but now it carries a sharper meaning: a person standing before several doors — Corporation, Nonprofit, Government, Small Business. Each door looks different from the outside. Open any of them and the interior is the same: the architectural skeleton. The doors are instances. The interior is the architecture.

This prework studies one instance in depth: US GAAP applied to a for-profit corporation (CVS Health). But the pedagogical goal is to teach the architecture, not the instance. Whenever a GAAP-specific rule appears, it will be identified as an instance choice — a decision that could have been made differently, and that *is* made differently in other systems. The student's job is to learn the architecture well enough that encountering a new instance feels like reading a new facade on a familiar building, not like starting from scratch.

### Move 4 (implicit, never stated): The System Works With You, Not For You

This move is never made explicitly — it is conveyed by the tone and structure of everything that follows. But it should be in the METALS team's minds as a design principle.

Because the system exists to manage complexity, it must compress. Compression requires choices. Choices involve tradeoffs. Every accounting number is the product of a chain of tradeoffs — between verifiability and relevance, between completeness and tractability, between timeliness and reliability. The system does not produce "the answer." It produces a structured, disciplined, internally consistent representation of economic reality that a thoughtful human can work with.

The student's role is not to receive the system's outputs passively. It is to read those outputs with understanding — knowing what conventions produced them, what tradeoffs shaped them, and what the system cannot show. This is what makes accounting intellectually serious: not the rules, but the judgment required to use the rules' outputs well.

The prework should convey this through its approach to every topic: never presenting a rule without explaining the tradeoff it reflects, never presenting a number without asking what it captures and what it omits. By the end, the student should instinctively ask those questions — not because we told them to, but because the material trained them to.

### Rick's Intro Video — Redone

The new intro video should:

- Welcome students from any background and institution
- Introduce the complexity argument in plain, concrete language (lemonade stand vs. CVS)
- Name the thesis: accounting is an information system — inputs, processing, outputs, users
- Introduce the architecture/instance distinction with one vivid example
- Preview the course diagram — showing the student where they are headed
- Give explicit guidance on the background quizzes — what to skip, what to engage with, and why
- Set expectations: this prework is designed so that students with no background and students with some background can both use it effectively

The video should be short — 3 to 5 minutes. Its job is orientation and motivation, not content delivery. The intellectual substance lives in the modules; the video is the map, not the territory.

### The Course Roadmap

The roadmap should be visual — not a numbered list of modules, but a diagram that conveys the logic of the sequence as a walk through an information system:

- Module 1: Orientation — you are here
- Module 2: Why the system exists — the users and purposes it serves
- Module 3: How the system processes — conventions, tradeoffs, and the limits of what it can see
- Module 4: The engine — the accounting identity and the four output channels
- Modules 5–7: One instance in action — CVS Health under US GAAP
- Module 8: The input language — debits, credits, journal entries, T-accounts

The student should see that the sequence moves from the outside in: first why the system exists, then how it works, then what its outputs look like in practice, then how inputs enter. By the end they will have walked the entire system.

### Background Quizzes — Redesigned

The background quizzes currently exist but lack clear instructions about who should do what. The redesign should:

- Explicitly tell students what the quiz is for — it is a diagnostic, not a test. There is no grade.
- Give explicit skip instructions — students with prior accounting exposure should be told which sections they can move through quickly, and which ones introduce genuinely new framing even for experienced students.
- Make the quiz adaptive if possible — a student who answers the first three questions correctly should be routed differently than one who struggles with them.
- Reframe what 'background' means — probe quantitative comfort and organizational familiarity, not just prior accounting knowledge.

Important note for the METALS team: even students with prior accounting courses should not skip the framing sections. The information system perspective, the architecture/instance distinction, and the complexity-and-tradeoffs framing will be new to virtually everyone — including students who have taken intermediate accounting. These are not review modules. They are reframing modules.

### Note on Geert

The reference to Geert should be removed entirely from the student-facing materials. If there is institutional context that needs to be preserved for Yale-specific deployments, it can live in a separate instructor note or a configurable module header — not in the main student experience.
