# Learning Objectives for Assessment Development

## CMU METALS Prework Redesign

*Revised March 22, 2026 — reflects completed one-by-one title interview with Rick Antle*

*These 13 objectives are organized into six groups. Each objective has a finalized title that states the broader capability the student should develop — not the specific assessment example. Each objective includes a concrete assessment design and a content support assessment indicating how well the current prework material supports it.*

*Numbering history: The original 19 objectives were first reduced to 18 (dropping original 3), then to 13 through a combination of further drops, combinations, and one addition. This document uses the final 1–13 numbering.*

## I. Double-entry Accounting as an Evolved Information System Architecture

### Objective 1 — Appreciate that different conventions arise in response to different problems and that none should be judged as inherently right or wrong.

**Assessment design:** Present a scenario — an entity holds an investment purchased for $80,000, now worth $120,000, not sold. Convention A recognizes the $40,000 gain on this year's income statement. Convention B recognizes no gain until the entity sells. Three-part question: (1) What's different about the financial statements? (2) Is one right and the other wrong? (3) What does the existence of both tell you about what an income statement is?

**Key insight:** The "which one is right" question is the real discriminator. The student who answers "neither — they're both convention choices" has internalized the objective.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 3 §2 presents historical cost vs. fair worth on the same building and makes the explicit point that "neither approach is wrong." The assessment scenario fits the same pattern.

---

### Objective 2 — Show that the identity holds under different conventions and distinguish architecture from instance.

**Assessment design:** An entity spends $50,000 on product development. Under System A, it's recorded as an asset. Under System B, it's expensed immediately. Show that A − L = E holds in both cases. Explain what's different (the convention choice) and what's the same (the architecture).

**Key insight:** The student is *doing* the architecture/instance distinction rather than naming it.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 3's two-convention comparison and Module 4's identity material provide the foundation. The student has the tools to work through this even though the specific R&D example isn't used in the prework.

## II. The Entity and Its Boundary

### Objective 3 — Understand the importance of the entity boundary decision and recognize that boundaries are drawn differently for different purposes.

**Assessment design:** Subsidiary A sells $10,000 of inventory to Subsidiary B for cash. Show effects on A's identity, effects on B's identity. Then draw the boundary around the consolidated group — what are the effects on the group's identity? Answer: none, because nothing crossed the group's boundary. The cash and inventory moved within the boundary.

**Key insight:** Frame as two legal entities owned by the same parent transacting with each other. Two separate identities change; the consolidated identity does not. This is the boundary concept demonstrated, not defined.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** The entity boundary exercise has a rabbit hole on consolidation that covers exactly this concept — parent with subsidiaries, intercompany transactions disappearing at the group level. But it's in a rabbit hole (not the main flow), and it's exposition rather than something the student works through. The concept is present; the operational practice is not.

---

### Objective 4 — Appreciate what factors influence whether economic information is recognized by the system.

**Assessment format: Multiple choice.** The entity has a purchased patent and an internally developed workforce. Why does the patent appear on the balance sheet and the workforce does not? Choices:
- The patent is more valuable than the workforce (wrong — recognition isn't about importance)
- The patent was acquired in a transaction at a verifiable amount; the workforce was not (correct)
- The patent is a physical asset; the workforce is not (wrong — assets are rights, not physical things)
- The system only records things that can be sold (wrong)

Follow-up: What does this mean for someone reading the balance sheet? Correct answer: the balance sheet may omit some of the entity's most important economic resources.

**Key insight:** Students at this stage can reason about alternatives put in front of them; they can't generate the reasoning from scratch. Multiple choice is the right format.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The entity boundary exercise and Module 4 §2 both cover recognition as the gatekeeper. The patent-vs-workforce distinction is consistent with what's taught. Module 3 also discusses what the system omits.

## III. Conventions, Worth, and Limits

### Objective 5 — Appreciate how different conventions serve different purposes and involve different tradeoffs.

**Assessment format: Multiple choice.** Capitalize R&D vs. expense R&D. What is a strength of capitalizing? (Reflects the entity's expectation of future benefits.) What is a strength of expensing? (Less costly to audit.)

**Key insight:** Expensing is less costly to audit because it eliminates the prediction-laden asset and all the ongoing audit work that asset generates.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** Module 3 presents two conventions on the same asset (historical cost vs. fair worth) and discusses their tradeoffs. But the specific framing of "less costly to audit" as a strength of expensing — and the underlying logic of why audit costs vary with convention choice — is not in the prework. **Needed:** A treatment of how convention choices affect audit costs, probably in Module 3 §3 (context) where oversight is already discussed.

---

### Objective 6 — Recognize the factors that influence accounting treatment.

**Assessment design:** Present a set of factors — information most useful for a given purpose, production cost (e.g., verification cost), auditability, comparability, and others still to be identified — and ask the student to explain how each one pushes toward a particular accounting treatment. For example: "An entity's internally developed brand is worth billions according to market analysts. Why doesn't it appear on the balance sheet? Which factors explain this?" The student should identify verifiability (no arm's-length transaction), production cost (what number would you put on it?), and the tradeoff between capturing something important and capturing something reliable.

**Key insight:** No single factor determines treatment. Accounting treatment reflects a balance among competing considerations. The student who can identify multiple factors and explain the tension between them has internalized this objective.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — BRAINSTORM NEEDED.** Module 3 discusses conventions and tradeoffs. Module 4 §2 covers recognition. But the full set of factors that influence accounting treatment has not been identified or organized. Two factors are identified so far: information most useful for a given purpose, and production cost (e.g., verification). The complete set still needs to be brainstormed.

## IV. The Identity and the Architecture

### Objective 7 — Appreciate the usefulness of imposing an identity, and recognize that it requires classification decisions — such as whether spending creates an asset or an expense.

**Assessment design (two parts):**

**(1) Identity as constraint.** After a transaction is recorded, an accountant notices that total assets don't equal total liabilities plus equities. What does this tell you? Multiple choice:
- The transaction was unusual and doesn't fit the system (wrong)
- An error was made in recording the transaction (correct)
- The identity only holds approximately (wrong)
- Some transactions affect only one side of the identity (wrong)

**(2) Classification.** Three cash outflows of $50,000 each — equipment purchase, this month's rent, R&D spending. Which is an asset, which is an expense? What's the difference? Equipment: clearest asset (predictable useful life). Rent: clearest expense (benefit consumed this month). R&D: contested middle ground — asset or expense depending on whether future benefit is sufficiently predictable.

**Key insight:** The identity is a constraint imposed on the system (not derived or discovered), and that constraint requires classification decisions. The asset-vs-expense question is the most fundamental such decision. Before a student can work with the identity, they should understand what determines whether spending creates an asset or an expense.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §1 opens with the identity as imposed — "Not discover one. Not derive one from economic theory. Impose one." But the asset-vs-expense classification exercise is not in the prework. The capitalize-vs-expense idea appears in Module 3's convention discussion as a passing reference, but not as an exercise in classification. **Needed:** A short treatment after the definitions in Module 4 §1, or as a bridge between Module 3 and Module 4, that asks: "You spent $50,000 — is it an asset or an expense? What determines the answer?"

---

### Objective 8 — Explain why the architecture applies to any entity and how the residual decomposition changes.

**Assessment design:** Side-by-side: a corporation with equities $119,000 (CC $100K / RE $19K) and a nonprofit hospital with net assets $119,000 (unrestricted $95K / restricted $24K). What's the same? What's different? Why does the decomposition differ?

**Key insight:** The nonprofit case is the strongest demonstration that the system is about information, not about quantifying some single property. A system that quantified a single property wouldn't work the same way on two fundamentally different organizations. But an information system whose architecture is general enough to apply to any entity with resources and obligations — that transfers.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §1 makes the point that the architecture transfers to nonprofits. But the assessment requires the student to compare specific decomposition categories (CC/RE vs. restricted/unrestricted), and the restricted/unrestricted distinction is not in the prework. **Needed:** A brief treatment of nonprofit net asset categories — even two sentences would be enough.

---

### Objective 9 — Start to experience how the identity is helpful in understanding transactions and events.

**Assessment design:** Give the student a new transaction and ask them to trace its effects — which accounts are affected, in which direction, and verify that the identity holds. The prepaid insurance example (pay $3,000 for six months of insurance — what accounts change?) was suggested but not finalized.

**Key insight:** "Understanding" not "analyzing" — "analyzing" invites the accountant perspective, which is not what the prework is building. The student is using the identity to make sense of what happened, not to apply a technique.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer provides direct practice with this skill.

## V. From Entries to Statements

### Objective 10 — Recognize that each entry captures what happened and why, but that understanding what the financial statements reveal requires knowing how they were put together.

**Assessment design:** Present two entries side by side — one that decreases cash and increases equipment (straightforward), and one that records depreciation expense (convention-driven). Then show the income statement that results from aggregating many such entries. Ask: "When you looked at each entry individually, the cause and effect were clear. Now that they're aggregated into the income statement, what additional knowledge do you need to interpret the number?" The student should recognize: you need to know which conventions were applied, because the aggregated number absorbs convention-driven adjustments that are invisible in the total.

**Key insight:** This objective names the gap between the transparency of individual entries and the opacity of aggregated statements. Aggregating cash stays concrete (you just add it up). Aggregating into retained earnings absorbs depreciation methods, recognition timing, estimation choices. This motivates why decomposition matters and why understanding conventions is essential for reading statements.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer lets students watch entries accumulate into statements, so the gap is experienced. But the explicit point — that aggregation absorbs convention choices and makes statements harder to interpret than individual entries — is not made in the current content. **Needed:** A brief treatment, possibly as a bridge between the transaction sequencer and the decomposition material.

---

### Objective 11 — Understand how decomposition can be used to add information.

**Assessment design (two parts):**

**(1) Equities decomposition.** After all eight transactions, total equities is $119,000 (from A − L = E). Given that you already know the total, what do you learn from seeing it broken into Contributed Capital $100,000 and Retained Earnings $19,000? For a nonprofit with the same total: what do you learn from restricted $24,000 vs. unrestricted $95,000?

**(2) Balance sheet change.** Present two balance sheets showing retained earnings of $100,000 at the start and $119,000 at the end. Ask: "The change in retained earnings is $19,000. Which of the following can you determine from that number alone?"
- (a) The entity earned $19,000 from operations — distractor: assumes no dividends
- (b) The entity was profitable — distractor: true but incomplete
- (c) The entity's equities increased by $19,000 from retained activities, but you cannot tell how much came from operations versus how much was paid out — correct
- (d) The entity collected $19,000 in cash — distractor: confuses accrual with cash

Follow-up: "What additional information does the income statement provide that the $19,000 change does not?"

**Key insight:** If you already know total equities (from A − L = E), the equities section tells you nothing new *unless* it is decomposed. If you already know the change in retained earnings, the income statement tells you nothing new *unless* it decomposes that change. Decomposition is where the information is. Frame positively: the decomposition statements exist because decomposing a change is an opportunity to add information.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §3 covers the balance-sheet-change leg directly. The equities decomposition as deliberate information design is not made explicit. **Needed:** A short addition to Module 4 §1 drawing out the information design point, plus a brief mention of restricted vs. unrestricted categories in the nonprofit passage.

---

### Objective 12 — Understand the structure of each decomposition — what change it explains, what categories it uses, and how the income statement nests within the statement of changes in equities.

**Assessment design:**

**(1) Matching with justification.** Give the student the opening and closing balance sheets. Three changes are visible: total equities moved from X to Y, cash moved from A to B, and within equities, retained earnings moved from M to N. For each, name the decomposition statement that explains it, and explain what information the decomposition adds that the bare change does not.

Expected answers:
- Statement of changes in equities decomposes the full equities change. The change alone is one number; the decomposition shows how much came from operations (retained earnings) versus new contributions versus other sources.
- Cash flow statement decomposes the change in cash. The change alone tells you cash went up or down; the decomposition shows how much came from operations versus investing versus financing.
- Income statement decomposes the operating component of the equities change — the part that runs through retained earnings. Revenue minus expenses is one number; the decomposition shows where the income came from and what was spent.

**(2) Nesting question (multiple choice).** "Net income appears as a single line in the statement of changes in equities. The income statement breaks that line into revenues and expenses. What does this tell you about the relationship between the two statements?"
- (a) They are independent reports that happen to share a number — wrong
- (b) The income statement is a more detailed breakdown of one component within the equities statement — correct
- (c) The equities statement summarizes the income statement — wrong: reverses the direction
- (d) Net income drives the change in equities, so the income statement is the primary report — wrong: implies a hierarchy that isn't there

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §3 describes all three decomposition statements and their relationships. The nesting is implicit in the current content. Making it more explicit would strengthen the support.

## VI. Reading the Statements Together

### Objective 13 — Read the financial statements as a connected sequence of decompositions, where each one adds information the previous one could not provide.

**Assessment design:** Present the eight-transaction entity. Opening retained earnings: $0. Closing retained earnings: $19,000.

**(1)** "The change in retained earnings is $19,000. Pick the best answer for what you can determine from that number alone."
- (a) The entity earned $19,000 from operations — wrong: assumes no dividends, but there was one
- (b) The entity was profitable — true but incomplete: if retained earnings increased, net income must have been positive. But you don't know how much — net income was actually $24,000, with a $5,000 dividend declared and paid. The single number doesn't tell you that.
- (c) Retained earnings increased by $19,000, but you cannot tell how much the entity earned or how much it paid out — you know it was profitable, but not how profitable — best answer
- (d) The entity collected $19,000 in cash — wrong: confuses accrual with cash

**(2)** "Now look at the income statement. Revenues were $30,000, expenses were $11,000, net income $19,000, no dividends. What did the income statement add that the $19,000 alone did not tell you?"

The student should recognize: it told you *how* the $19,000 got there — how much came in as revenue, how much went out as expenses. It decomposed the change.

**(3)** "Now compare: net income is $19,000 but cash from operations is a different number. What does this comparison add that the income statement alone did not tell you?"

The student should recognize: it tells you how much of the income story was backed by cash versus shaped by conventions. The income statement told you how the retained earnings changed; the cash flow statement tells you how much of that story actually moved cash.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer produces all the data needed — opening and closing retained earnings, the dividend, net income, and cash from operations.

## Assessment Design Patterns

Three consistent patterns emerged across all objectives:

1. **Two conventions, same event:** Present two alternatives, ask what differs, ask which is right. The student who says "neither — they're different conventions" has internalized the central insight. (Objectives 1, 2, 5)

2. **Multiple choice with distractor misconceptions:** For students at this stage who can reason about alternatives put in front of them but can't generate specific examples from scratch. (Objectives 4, 5, 7, 12)

3. **Work the identity:** Give a scenario, ask the student to trace effects and verify balance. (Objectives 3, 7, 9)

## Notes for the CMU Team

**On what changed in the revision:** The original objectives were largely "explain why" prompts. The restated versions are built around assessable student behavior — "given this scenario, show what happens" or "given these alternatives, identify which and explain why." This makes them testable without requiring students to generate philosophical arguments from scratch.

**On the "which one is right?" question:** Several objectives use the pattern of presenting two conventions and asking which is correct. The answer — "neither, they're both convention choices" — is the central insight of the prework. A student who can give that answer and explain it has demonstrated understanding of the system as an evolved information system whose features reflect convention choices, not natural facts.

**On multiple choice:** Multiple choice is appropriate for students at this stage on many objectives. They can reason about alternatives put in front of them; they can't yet generate specific examples from scratch. The distractors in each objective are designed around common misconceptions (e.g., that recognition is about importance rather than verifiability, that the identity only holds approximately, that cash is important because it's the "most important" asset).

**On what the prework does not aim to do:** The prework does not aim to make students proficient at preparing financial statements, performing ratio analysis, or applying specific GAAP or IFRS rules. It aims to give them the structural understanding that makes the first accounting course intelligible — so that when they encounter debits and credits, journal entries, and specific recognition rules, they know what the system is, why it was built that way, and what questions to ask about any number it produces.

**On the "experience first, name later" principle:** Several objectives are introduced experientially before being named explicitly. The transaction sequencer lets the student watch accounts appear, the identity hold, and the NI/CFO gap emerge before anyone explains what those phenomena mean. The reflection prompts then ask the student to articulate what they observed. Assessment should respect this sequence: test whether the student can explain what they experienced, not whether they can recite definitions they were told.

**On dropped objectives:** Four objectives from the earlier 18-objective list were dropped as too ambitious for prework: tracing specific sources of the NI-vs-CFO gap, interpreting persistent multi-period NI-vs-CFO patterns, ranking items by convention-dependence, and a standalone objective on why cash gets its own statement. These are course-level skills. The structural points they carried are subsumed by Objective 12 (structure of each decomposition) and Objective 13 (reading the statements together as connected decompositions).
