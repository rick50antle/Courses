# Learning Objectives for Assessment Development (Revised)

## CMU METALS Prework Redesign

*Revised March 21, 2026 — reflects one-by-one review with Rick Antle*

*These objectives have been restated from the original 19 to focus on assessable student behavior rather than abstract "explain why" prompts. Each objective now includes a concrete assessment design. Original Objective 3 was dropped; a new Objective 8 was added. The numbering below is the revised numbering.*

*Each objective includes a content support assessment: how well the current prework material supports the objective as restated. Objectives flagged as needing new content cannot be fairly assessed until that content is added.*

## I. The System as an Evolved Information System

### Objective 1 — Two conventions, same event, neither is wrong

**Restated:** Given two conventions applied to the same economic event, explain why they produce different financial representations and why neither is inherently wrong.

**Assessment design:** Present a scenario — an entity holds an investment purchased for $80,000, now worth $120,000, not sold. Convention A recognizes the $40,000 gain on this year's income statement. Convention B recognizes no gain until the entity sells. Three-part question: (1) What's different about the financial statements? (2) Is one right and the other wrong? (3) What does the existence of both tell you about what an income statement is?

**Key insight:** The "which one is right" question is the real discriminator. The student who answers "neither — they're both design choices" has internalized the objective.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 3 §2 presents historical cost vs. fair worth on the same building and makes the explicit point that "neither approach is wrong." The assessment scenario fits the same pattern.

---

### Objective 2 — Same event, two systems, identity holds in both

**Restated:** Given the same economic event recorded under two different sets of conventions, show that the identity holds in both cases and explain what differs and why.

**Assessment design:** An entity spends $50,000 on product development. Under System A, it's recorded as an asset. Under System B, it's expensed immediately. Show that A − L = E holds in both cases. Explain what's different (the convention choice) and what's the same (the architecture).

**Key insight:** The student is *doing* the architecture/instance distinction rather than naming it.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 3's two-convention comparison and Module 4's identity material provide the foundation. The student has the tools to work through this even though the specific R&D example isn't used in the prework.

---

### Objective 3 — DROPPED

Original asked students to explain why the prework uses IT vocabulary. This asks about a pedagogical choice, not about understanding the system. The concept it carried (understanding what "instance" means) is demonstrated through Objective 2.

## II. The Entity and Its Boundary

### Objective 4 — Entity as boundary, intercompany transactions

**Restated:** Given a transaction between two entities within a larger group, show its effects on each entity's identity separately, and explain why it has no effect on the consolidated entity's identity.

**Assessment design:** Subsidiary A sells $10,000 of inventory to Subsidiary B for cash. Show effects on A's identity, effects on B's identity. Then draw the boundary around the consolidated group — what are the effects on the group's identity? Answer: none, because nothing crossed the group's boundary. The cash and inventory moved within the boundary.

**Key insight:** Frame as two legal entities owned by the same parent transacting with each other. Two separate identities change; the consolidated identity does not. This is the boundary concept demonstrated, not defined.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED.** The entity boundary exercise has a rabbit hole on consolidation that covers exactly this concept — parent with subsidiaries, intercompany transactions disappearing at the group level. But it's in a rabbit hole (not the main flow), and it's exposition rather than something the student works through. The concept is present; the operational practice is not.

---

### Objective 5 — Recognition as gatekeeper

**Restated:** Given examples of economic resources that are and are not recorded by the system, identify what determines whether something enters the system and recognize what this means for interpreting the balance sheet.

**Assessment format: Multiple choice.** The entity has a purchased patent and an internally developed workforce. Why does the patent appear on the balance sheet and the workforce does not? Choices:
- The patent is more valuable than the workforce (wrong — recognition isn't about importance)
- The patent was acquired in a transaction at a verifiable amount; the workforce was not (correct)
- The patent is a physical asset; the workforce is not (wrong — assets are rights, not physical things)
- The system only records things that can be sold (wrong)

Follow-up: What does this mean for someone reading the balance sheet? Correct answer: the balance sheet may omit some of the entity's most important economic resources.

**Key insight:** Students at this stage can reason about alternatives put in front of them; they can't generate the reasoning from scratch. Multiple choice is the right format.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The entity boundary exercise and Module 4 §2 both cover recognition as the gatekeeper. The patent-vs-workforce distinction is consistent with what's taught. Module 3 also discusses what the system omits.

## III. Conventions, Worth, and Limits

### Objective 6 — Two conventions, strengths and weaknesses

**Restated:** Given two conventions applied to the same event, identify the relative strengths and weaknesses of each.

**Assessment format: Multiple choice.** Capitalize R&D vs. expense R&D. What is a strength of capitalizing? (Reflects the entity's expectation of future benefits.) What is a strength of expensing? (Less costly to audit.)

**Key insight:** Expensing is less costly to audit because it eliminates the prediction-laden asset and all the ongoing audit work that asset generates.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** Module 3 presents two conventions on the same asset (historical cost vs. fair worth) and discusses their tradeoffs. But the specific framing of "less costly to audit" as a strength of expensing — and the underlying logic of why audit costs vary with convention choice — is not in the prework. The audit cost idea was identified this session as new content. **Needed:** A treatment of how convention choices affect audit costs, probably in Module 3 §3 (context) where oversight is already discussed.

---

### Objective 7 — Predictions, audit costs, and information asymmetry

**Restated:** Identify the predictions embedded in balance sheet items, explain why those predictions are a source of uncertainty in the numbers, and explain why they generate audit costs — including the information asymmetry between managers and auditors.

**Assessment design:** Give the student three items — cash, a receivable, capitalized R&D. Rank by how much audit work they require and explain why. The ranking follows the degree of prediction each one requires. Cash: just count it. Receivable: predict whether the customer will pay. Capitalized R&D: predict whether the project will produce any benefit at all.

**Key insights:**
1. Hidden in the definition of assets and liabilities is the need to make a prediction. "Present rights to future economic benefits" — but with R&D, is there a benefit at all?
2. Every prediction on the balance sheet generates audit costs. The further from cash you move, the more audit work.
3. The manager has better information than the auditor. If the manager wants to capitalize and the auditor thinks it should be expensed, they negotiate — and the auditor is at an informational disadvantage.
4. A convention that creates more room for managerial discretion is a convention that generates more audit cost, precisely because of the information asymmetry.

**Content support: NOT SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** The manager-auditor information asymmetry is not in the prework. The idea that predictions are embedded in asset/liability definitions is not made explicit. Module 3 §3 discusses context, incentives, and the existence of auditors, but does not develop the connection between convention choice, prediction, and audit cost. **Needed:** (1) A treatment of predictions embedded in definitions, probably in Module 4 §1 after the definitions of assets and liabilities. (2) A treatment of manager-auditor information asymmetry and how convention choices affect audit costs, probably in Module 3 §3 or as a rabbit hole in Module 4.

---

### Objective 8 (NEW) — Classify cash outflows as assets or expenses

**Restated:** Given several cash outflows, classify each as creating an asset or incurring an expense, and explain what determines the difference.

**Assessment design:** Three cash outflows of $50,000 each — equipment purchase, this month's rent, R&D spending. Which is an asset, which is an expense? What's the difference? Equipment: clearest asset (predictable useful life). Rent: clearest expense (benefit consumed this month). R&D: contested middle ground — asset or expense depending on whether future benefit is sufficiently predictable.

**Key insight:** This is more fundamental than the structural objectives about the identity. Before a student can work with the identity, they should understand what determines whether spending creates an asset or an expense.

**Content support: NOT SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** Module 4 §1 defines assets and liabilities, and Module 4 §2 mentions recognition. But the prework never explicitly asks whether a cash outflow created an asset or an expense, and never walks through what determines the difference. The capitalize-vs-expense idea appears in Module 3's convention discussion as a passing reference, but not as an exercise in classification. **Needed:** A short treatment in Module 4 §1 after the definitions, or as a bridge between Module 3 and Module 4, that asks: "You spent $50,000 — is it an asset or an expense? What determines the answer?"

## IV. The Identity and the Architecture

### Objective 9 — Identity is imposed

**Restated:** Recognize that the accounting identity is a constraint imposed on the information system, not a description of a natural relationship, and explain what the constraint makes possible — including self-balancing and error detection.

**Assessment design:** After a transaction is recorded, an accountant notices that total assets don't equal total liabilities plus equities. What does this tell you? Multiple choice:
- The transaction was unusual and doesn't fit the system (wrong)
- An error was made in recording the transaction (correct)
- The identity only holds approximately (wrong)
- Some transactions affect only one side of the identity (wrong)

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §1 opens with exactly this point — "Not discover one. Not derive one from economic theory. Impose one."

---

### Objective 10 — Equities decomposition as information design

**Restated:** Explain that total equities is determined by the asset and liability recordings, and that the decomposition of equities into components (contributed capital vs. retained earnings; restricted vs. unrestricted net assets) is a deliberate information design choice that reveals how the residual got there.

**Assessment design:** After all eight transactions, total equities is $119,000 (from A − L = E). Given that you already know the total, what do you learn from seeing it broken into Contributed Capital $100,000 and Retained Earnings $19,000? For a nonprofit with the same total: what do you learn from restricted $24,000 vs. unrestricted $95,000?

**Key insight:** If you know total assets and total liabilities, you already know total equities — it's just arithmetic. For the equities section to tell you anything new, there have to be details. The decomposition is where the information is.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** Module 4 §1 establishes equities as the difference and mentions that for nonprofits you relabel as "net assets." The transaction sequencer shows contributed capital and retained earnings. But the *point* — that the decomposition is a deliberate information design choice, and that the total alone tells you nothing new — is not made explicitly. And **restricted vs. unrestricted net assets are not presented at all.** **Needed:** (1) A short addition to Module 4 §1 drawing out the information design point about the equities decomposition. (2) A brief mention of restricted vs. unrestricted categories in the nonprofit passage — even two sentences would be enough.

---

### Objective 11 — Architecture works for any entity

**Restated:** Explain why the same architecture applies to any entity with resources and obligations — for-profit, nonprofit, government — and how the decomposition of the residual changes to serve the information needs of different users.

**Assessment design:** Side-by-side: a corporation with equities $119,000 (CC $100K / RE $19K) and a nonprofit hospital with net assets $119,000 (unrestricted $95K / restricted $24K). What's the same? What's different? Why does the decomposition differ?

**Key insight:** The nonprofit case is the strongest demonstration that the system is about information, not measurement. A measurement instrument wouldn't work the same way on two fundamentally different organizations.

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — draws on same new content as Objective 10.** Module 4 §1 makes the point that the architecture transfers to nonprofits. But the assessment requires the student to compare specific decomposition categories (CC/RE vs. restricted/unrestricted), and the restricted/unrestricted distinction is not in the prework. **Needed:** Same addition as Objective 10 — a brief treatment of nonprofit net asset categories.

---

### Objective 12 — Trace a transaction

**Restated:** Given a transaction, determine which accounts are affected, in which direction, and verify that the identity holds.

**Assessment design:** Give the student a new transaction and ask them to trace its effects. The prepaid insurance example (pay $3,000 for six months of insurance — what accounts change?) was suggested but not finalized.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer provides direct practice with this skill.

## V. Two Snapshots and Three Decompositions

### Objective 13 — Decomposition adds information

**Restated:** Explain why decomposing the change between two balance sheets adds information that the change alone does not provide.

**Assessment design:** Present two balance sheets showing retained earnings of $100,000 at the start and $119,000 at the end. Ask: "The change in retained earnings is $19,000. Which of the following can you determine from that number alone?"
- (a) The entity earned $19,000 from operations — distractor: assumes no dividends
- (b) The entity was profitable — distractor: true but incomplete; $19K could reflect operations plus dividends netting to $19K
- (c) The entity's equities increased by $19,000 from retained activities, but you cannot tell how much came from operations versus how much was paid out — correct
- (d) The entity collected $19,000 in cash — distractor: confuses accrual with cash

Follow-up: "What additional information does the income statement provide that the $19,000 change does not?"

**Key insight:** Frame positively. The flow statements exist because decomposing a change is an opportunity to add information.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §3 covers this directly.

---

### Objective 14 — What each flow statement decomposes, and the nesting relationship

**Restated:** Given opening and closing balance sheets, identify which specific change each flow statement decomposes, explain why each decomposition adds information the balance sheet change alone does not provide, and explain the nesting relationship — that the income statement decomposes a component that appears as a single line within the statement of changes in equities.

**Assessment design:**

**(1) Matching with justification.** Give the student the opening and closing balance sheets. Three changes are visible: total equities moved from X to Y, cash moved from A to B, and within equities, retained earnings moved from M to N. For each, name the flow statement that decomposes it, and explain what information the decomposition adds that the bare change does not.

Expected answers:
- Statement of changes in equities decomposes the full equities change. The change alone is one number; the decomposition shows how much came from operations (retained earnings) versus new contributions versus other sources.
- Cash flow statement decomposes the change in cash. The change alone tells you cash went up or down; the decomposition shows how much came from operations versus investing versus financing.
- Income statement decomposes the operating component of the equities change — the part that flows through retained earnings. Revenue minus expenses is one number; the decomposition shows where the income came from and what was spent.

**(2) Nesting question (multiple choice).** "Net income appears as a single line in the statement of changes in equities. The income statement breaks that line into revenues and expenses. What does this tell you about the relationship between the two statements?"
- (a) They are independent reports that happen to share a number — wrong
- (b) The income statement is a more detailed breakdown of one component within the equities statement — correct
- (c) The equities statement summarizes the income statement — wrong: reverses the direction
- (d) Net income drives the change in equities, so the income statement is the primary report — wrong: implies a hierarchy that isn't there

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** Module 4 §3 describes all three flow statements and their relationships. The nesting is implicit in the current content. Making it more explicit would strengthen the support.

---

### Objective 15 — Why cash gets its own statement

**Restated:** Explain what makes cash different from every other asset on the balance sheet, and explain what comparing cash from operations to net income reveals about the role of conventions in the income statement.

**Assessment design (two parts):**

**(1) Multiple choice.** "The system gives cash its own flow statement. What makes cash different enough to justify this?" (Select the two strongest reasons.)
- (a) Cash is the most important asset — wrong: importance isn't the structural distinction
- (b) Cash is the only item whose balance is determined by counting, with no conventions about recognition timing, useful lives, or estimates — comparing it to net income reveals how much of the income statement is shaped by conventions — correct
- (c) Cash is the most liquid asset and the most portable, making it the biggest target for theft — tracking where it came from and where it went serves the system's oversight function — correct
- (d) Cash is the only asset that appears on both the balance sheet and the cash flow statement — wrong: confuses the decomposition relationship with a property of cash

**(2) Interpretive question.** Present the eight-transaction entity's results: net income and cash from operations differ. Why are these two numbers different, and what does the difference tell you about the income statement?

**Follow-up (multiple choice).** "If net income and cash from operations were always identical, what would that tell you about the system?"
- (a) The system is working correctly — wrong: the gap is not a malfunction
- (b) The entity has no accruals, meaning every revenue and expense was settled in cash immediately — correct
- (c) The conventions don't matter for this entity — wrong: even a cash-basis entity is applying a convention
- (d) The cash flow statement would be unnecessary — wrong: the operating/investing/financing decomposition would still add information for oversight

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — NEW CONTENT NEEDED.** Module 4 §3 covers cash as the convention-free benchmark — "the one item where the comparison to the income statement is particularly revealing." That leg is supported. But the second leg — cash as the asset most vulnerable to theft, and tracking its movements as an oversight function — **is not in the prework at all.** Module 3 §3 discusses oversight, incentives, and the existence of auditors, which provides a natural home. **Needed:** A brief addition (even a paragraph) connecting cash tracking to the safeguarding of assets, probably in Module 3 §3 or Module 4 §3.

## VI. Reading the Statements Together

### Objective 16 — NI vs. CFO gap: trace it

**Restated:** Given a set of transactions, trace the specific sources of the gap between net income and cash from operations, and explain why each source produces a difference.

**Assessment design:**

**(1) Tracing exercise.** Use the eight-transaction entity. Present net income and cash from operations side by side. "Identify each transaction that contributes to the gap between net income and cash from operations. For each one, explain whether it increases or decreases the gap, and why."

**(2) Multiple choice.** "The gap between net income and cash from operations exists because:"
- (a) The income statement contains errors that the cash flow statement corrects — wrong: the gap is not an error
- (b) The income statement reflects recognition conventions while the cash flow statement reflects actual cash movement — the two are answering different questions about the same period — correct
- (c) The cash flow statement uses different conventions than the income statement — wrong: the CFS tracks cash, which requires no conventions
- (d) Some transactions are missing from one statement but not the other — wrong: same transactions, recorded differently

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer produces the gap. The Reflect 4 prompts ask students to trace it.

---

### Objective 17 — NI vs. CFO gap: interpret it

**Restated:** Given a gap between net income and cash from operations, interpret what the gap reveals about the entity's situation and identify the questions it should prompt.

**Assessment design:** Present two entities with the same net income — $50,000 each — but different cash from operations. Entity A: CFO $48,000. Entity B: CFO $12,000.

**(1) Interpretive question.** "Both entities report the same net income. What does the difference in cash from operations tell you about each one?"

**(2) Multiple choice.** "Entity B has reported $50,000 in net income but only $12,000 in cash from operations for three consecutive years. Which is the most important question this pattern should prompt?"
- (a) Is the entity reporting income it will never collect in cash? — correct: a persistent gap raises the question of whether the conventions are running ahead of economic reality
- (b) Is the entity paying too many dividends? — wrong: dividends don't appear in CFO
- (c) Should the entity switch to a different set of conventions? — wrong: the gap isn't a reason to change conventions; it's a reason to ask what the conventions are doing
- (d) Is the cash flow statement wrong? — wrong: the CFS is the convention-free number

**Content support: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — STRETCH OBJECTIVE.** The transaction sequencer shows one period's gap, which is enough to understand what the gap is. But the assessment presents a persistent pattern over three years and asks what questions it should prompt. The prework doesn't develop multi-period thinking. **The single-period version is supported; the persistent-pattern version is a stretch.** A brief addition — even one paragraph about what persistence in the gap means — would close this.

---

### Objective 18 — Convention spectrum (lightened)

**Restated:** Recognize that some financial statement items are shaped more by conventions than others, and that cash is the benchmark against which convention-dependent numbers can be compared.

**Assessment design (multiple choice).** "Here are three items from the balance sheet: cash, equipment (after depreciation), and accounts receivable. Which one involves the most convention-dependent judgment, and why?"
- (a) Cash — because the entity must decide which bank accounts to include — wrong: cash is just counted
- (b) Accounts receivable — because the entity must estimate how much will be collected — correct in part, but not the most
- (c) Equipment — because after the initial transaction, the number depends on choosing a depreciation method, a useful life, and a residual value, plus ongoing assessments of whether the asset is still generating the expected benefits. Multiple convention choices and predictions accumulate over time — correct
- (d) All three involve the same amount of judgment — wrong

**Follow-up.** "Given your answer, why is comparing cash from operations to net income informative?"

**Content support: MARGINALLY SUPPORTED.** Module 3 establishes that conventions shape numbers. The prework contrasts cash (count it) with items requiring estimates. The lightened version asks for a single discrimination rather than a full ranking. The student has the pieces but hasn't been asked to assemble them in quite this way. Probably assessable with current content, but close to the edge.

### Objective 19 — Read statements together, drilling down from retained earnings

**Restated:** Starting from the change in retained earnings between two balance sheets, explain what you learn by decomposing that change through the income statement, and what you learn by then comparing the income statement to the cash flow statement — demonstrating that each additional statement adds information the previous one could not provide.

**Assessment design:** Present the eight-transaction entity. Opening retained earnings: $0. Closing retained earnings: $19,000.

**(1)** "The change in retained earnings is $19,000. Pick the best answer for what you can determine from that number alone."
- (a) The entity earned $19,000 from operations — wrong: assumes no dividends, but there was one
- (b) The entity was profitable — true but incomplete: if retained earnings increased, net income must have been positive. But you don't know how much — net income was actually $24,000, with a $5,000 dividend declared and paid. The single number doesn't tell you that.
- (c) Retained earnings increased by $19,000, but you cannot tell how much the entity earned or how much it paid out — you know it was profitable, but not how profitable — best answer
- (d) The entity collected $19,000 in cash — wrong: confuses accrual with cash

**(2)** "Now look at the income statement. Revenues were $30,000, expenses were $11,000, net income $19,000, no dividends. What did the income statement add that the $19,000 alone did not tell you?"

The student should recognize: it told you *how* the $19,000 got there — how much came in as revenue, how much went out as expenses. It decomposed the change.

**(3)** "Now compare: net income is $19,000 but cash from operations is a different number. What does this comparison add that the income statement alone did not tell you?"

The student should recognize: it tells you how much of the income story was backed by cash versus shaped by conventions. The income statement told you how the retained earnings changed; the cash flow statement tells you how much of that story actually moved cash.

**Content support: SUPPORTED.** The transaction sequencer produces all the data needed — opening and closing retained earnings, the dividend, net income, and cash from operations.

## Assessment Design Patterns

Three consistent patterns emerged across all objectives:

1. **Two conventions, same event:** Present two alternatives, ask what differs, ask which is right. The student who says "neither — they're different conventions" has internalized the central insight. (Objectives 1, 2, 6)

2. **Multiple choice with distractor misconceptions:** For students at this stage who can reason about alternatives put in front of them but can't generate specific examples from scratch. (Objectives 5, 6, 9, 15, 18)

3. **Work the identity:** Give a scenario, ask the student to trace effects and verify balance. (Objectives 4, 8, 12)

## Notes for the CMU Team

**On what changed in the revision:** The original objectives were largely "explain why" prompts. The restated versions are built around assessable student behavior — "given this scenario, show what happens" or "given these alternatives, identify which and explain why." This makes them testable without requiring students to generate philosophical arguments from scratch.

**On the "which one is right?" question:** Several objectives use the pattern of presenting two conventions and asking which is correct. The answer — "neither, they're both design choices" — is the central insight of the prework. A student who can give that answer and explain it has demonstrated understanding of the system as an evolved information system whose features reflect convention choices, not natural facts.

**On multiple choice:** Multiple choice is appropriate for students at this stage on many objectives. They can reason about alternatives put in front of them; they can't yet generate specific examples from scratch. The distractors in each objective are designed around common misconceptions (e.g., that the gap between NI and CFO is an error, that cash is important because it's the "most important" asset, that recognition is about importance rather than verifiability).

**On what the prework does not aim to do:** The prework does not aim to make students proficient at preparing financial statements, performing ratio analysis, or applying specific GAAP or IFRS rules. It aims to give them the structural understanding that makes the first accounting course intelligible — so that when they encounter debits and credits, journal entries, and specific recognition rules, they know what the system is, why it was built that way, and what questions to ask about any number it produces.

**On the "experience first, name later" principle:** Several objectives are introduced experientially before being named explicitly. The transaction sequencer lets the student watch accounts appear, the identity hold, and the NI/CFO gap emerge before anyone explains what those phenomena mean. The reflection prompts then ask the student to articulate what they observed. Assessment should respect this sequence: test whether the student can explain what they experienced, not whether they can recite definitions they were told.
